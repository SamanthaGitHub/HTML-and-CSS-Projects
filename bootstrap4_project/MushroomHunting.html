Hunting for Mushrooms

A guide to help Oregon hunters gather edible mushrooms,
and avoid deadly look alikes

Dear Hunters:

The number of mushroom species is huge, and it's quite easy to confuse them with one another.

It's important to know where to hunt for the mushrooms you're seeking, and have a comprehensive identification book handy.

Identifying and correctly collecting mushrooms can help the hunter from serious illness, as well as leave a patch of promising hunting when they return again.

  • Southern Oregon Choice Species:
  • Chanterelle
  • Morel
  • Lion's Mane
  • Truffle

How to Hunt For:

Chanterelle

The chanterelle season in the Pacific Northwest begins as early as mid-summer at the higher elevation and lasts to the first frost, typically until November. Mixed hardwood forests are always the best place to find them, around oaks, maple, beech, poplar, and birch.


Summer Spring Warning

Morel

At the beginning of spring when the ground first warms, morels are likely to be found on south-facing slopes in fairly open areas where the sun reaches the ground. You'll want to seek out aspen, ash, oak, and elm trees, which have a symbiotic relationship with the mushroom.


Spring

Lion's Mane

Lion's Mane is found on decaying hardwood like oak, birch and walnut in the late summer and fall. Other places lions mane mushroom can be found is in the wounds of dying or dead trees. It is typically always found on hardwoods.


Summer Fall

Truffle

Both Oregon white truffle species and the Oregon black truffle species can be found in Douglas-fir forests. They can be found in mixed conifer forests as well. These mushrooms grow year-round.


Year-round
